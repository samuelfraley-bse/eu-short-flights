1. Introduction

This project evaluates the potential for high-speed rail (HSR) to substitute short-haul flights across major airports in western Europe. As aviation emissions become an increasing policy concern, high-speed rail has emerged as a promising lower-carbon alternative for short- and medium-distance travel. Europe provides an ideal setting for this analysis due to its dense transportation networks and high volume of intra-regional air traffic.

The central research question is: which short-haul European flights can realistically be replaced by high-speed rail, and under what conditions? To answer this, the analysis integrates spatial rail infrastructure data with airport-level passenger flows and applies both geographic and economic criteria to evaluate substitution feasibility.

2. Methodology

This study uses a spatial network analysis framework focused on the 15 largest hub airports in western Europe. The approach combines geographic proximity, travel time estimation, and cost modeling to identify substitution opportunities.

First, the European passenger rail network (GL2017) is mapped and classified into high-speed and conventional rail segments. Airports are then evaluated based on their proximity to high-speed rail infrastructure, using a 25 km threshold to define meaningful access.

Next, outbound air routes from each airport are evaluated. A route is classified as HSR-suitable if both endpoints are within 25 km of high-speed rail and the estimated rail travel time is under six hours.

2. Methodology (continued)

Rail travel times are approximated using simplified speeds based on infrastructure type, with high-speed rail assigned faster average speeds than conventional lines. While this approach abstracts from real-world routing and scheduling complexities, it provides a consistent and scalable method for comparing travel times across a large number of routes.

Finally, a carbon-adjusted cost framework is applied to compare rail and air travel. This incorporates standard estimates of emissions per passenger kilometer alongside a carbon price and value-of-time assumptions. Routes where rail is both time-competitive and lower in carbon-adjusted cost are identified as strong candidates for substitution.

3. European Rail Network Overview

The European passenger rail network reveals a highly uneven but strategically dense distribution of infrastructure across western Europe. High-speed rail corridors are concentrated in France, Spain, Germany, and northern Italy, forming a backbone of fast intercity connectivity. In contrast, conventional rail provides broader geographic coverage but at lower speeds.

Despite some classification limitations in the dataset, the network appears largely continuous across the study region. This suggests that most major airports are not geographically isolated from rail infrastructure, even if the quality and speed of service vary. This underlying connectivity is a necessary condition for any large-scale substitution of short-haul flights.

4. Airport Distribution and Demand

The spatial distribution of the top 15 western European hub airports closely aligns with the densest portions of the rail network. Major hubs such as London Heathrow, Paris Charles de Gaulle, and Amsterdam Schiphol dominate in terms of passenger volume and serve as central nodes in the European aviation system.

Importantly, these airports are located within regions that are already well-served by rail infrastructure. This geographic overlap between high air traffic demand and rail accessibility suggests strong potential for substitution, particularly for short-distance routes where rail travel times are competitive.

5. Airport Accessibility to High-Speed Rail

Most major airports are located within close proximity to the European rail network, indicating that physical access is not the primary constraint for substitution. Airports within 25 km of high-speed rail infrastructure are particularly well-positioned to integrate rail into passenger travel options.

However, key exceptions highlight the importance of last-mile connectivity. For example, London Heathrow lacks direct proximity to high-speed rail under the defined threshold, reflecting broader challenges in integrating airport infrastructure with national rail systems. These gaps suggest that infrastructure alone is not sufficient—connectivity and integration are equally critical.

6. Substitution Potential by Airport

Substitution potential varies significantly across airports, driven by both infrastructure access and route composition. Airports in central Europe, such as Frankfurt, Paris, and Amsterdam, exhibit the highest shares of passengers traveling on routes that meet both the accessibility and travel time criteria.

In contrast, airports such as Barcelona show lower substitution potential despite strong rail infrastructure, as a larger share of their routes extend beyond the six-hour threshold. Overall, the analysis finds that a substantial proportion of rail-connected routes fall within the time window where rail can realistically compete with air travel, though this varies considerably by location.

7. Carbon-Adjusted Cost Comparison

When incorporating carbon-adjusted costs, the number of routes where rail is strictly more efficient than air travel becomes more limited. At a baseline carbon price of €100 per tonne of CO₂, only a subset of routes—primarily within central European corridors—are identified as carbon-optimal for rail.

This finding highlights that while rail is generally lower-emission, it is not always cost-competitive under current pricing structures. The results suggest that environmental benefits alone may not be sufficient to drive widespread substitution without supportive policy measures.

8. Carbon Price Sensitivity Analysis

The sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the number of rail-favorable routes increases as carbon prices rise. The relationship is nonlinear, with stepwise increases as individual routes cross cost competitiveness thresholds.

At current EU ETS levels, relatively few routes qualify. However, as carbon prices approach projected 2030 levels, a substantially larger share of routes become viable for substitution. This indicates that policy changes, particularly in carbon pricing, could play a decisive role in shifting travel behavior.

9. Limitations

While this analysis provides a useful approximation of substitution potential, several limitations should be acknowledged.

First, the use of simple distance thresholds to measure rail accessibility does not capture the complexity of real-world connectivity, including station access, transfers, and passenger convenience. Second, the rail dataset is not fully up to date and may omit recent infrastructure developments or misclassify certain high-speed segments.

Third, travel time estimates are based on simplified assumptions rather than detailed network routing or timetable data, which may lead to inaccuracies in estimating competitiveness. Finally, both the air and rail datasets contain gaps and inconsistencies, meaning the results should be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive.

10. Conclusion

This study finds that high-speed rail has meaningful potential to replace a share of short-haul flights in western Europe, particularly in regions with dense infrastructure and high concentrations of short-distance routes. However, substitution potential is highly uneven and depends on both physical connectivity and economic conditions.

The results underscore the importance of policy, especially carbon pricing, in making rail more competitive with air travel. While infrastructure provides the foundation, pricing mechanisms and integration efforts will ultimately determine the extent to which modal substitution occurs. Future work could incorporate more detailed routing, scheduling data, and behavioral considerations to refine these estimates and better capture real-world travel decisions.
